REVIEWS


Reviewer: Ralph R. Martin

The SIGGRAPH annual conferences are undoubtedly the most prestigious and well-attended conferences in the field of computer graphics. There is much competition to present papers at SIGGRAPH conferences, so the standard of papers accepted and contained in this volume is expected to be high. The volume contains about 50 research papers; also included are short (typically two-page) reports of the approximately 20 panel sessions held at the conference. The research papers cover a wide range of computer graphics topics, and are grouped for convenience under the headings “Morphing,” “Efficient Polygonal Surfaces,” “Humans and Clothing,” “Mathematical Techniques,” “Free-form Surfaces and Deformation,” “Virtual Reality,” “Hardware and Multimedia,” “Rendering,” “Animation,” “Interactive Systems and Techniques,” and “Modelling.” On the other hand, the panels emphasize subjects such as standards, education, future developments, and uses of computer graphics. Commenting on every paper is obviously impossible, but some of the more stimulating ones for me were “Physically Based Morphing,” by Sederberg et al., “Algebraic Splines,” by Bajaj et al., “Reflectance,” by Westin et al., “Curved Reflectors,” by Mitchell et al., “Interpolation Using Quaternions,” by Barr et al., “Inspection of Solids,” by Rossignac et al., and “Spiral Phyllotaxis,” by Fowler et al. Nearly all of the papers are by authors from the US, who sometimes seem unaware of related research in the rest of the world. In particular, one paper on variational surface modeling claims this technique to be new, and does not refer to any of the many previous papers on variational curve and surface methods. Other papers based on interval arithmetic methods seem to suffer from a similar problem, although to a lesser extent. A trend that is increasingly noticeable in this year's proceedings is the large number of papers concerned with the creation of image sequences, rather than just a single image, and especially those based on the use of physical models of deforming objects. Improvements in modeling reflectance and radiosity are still of interest, but surprisingly few papers directly describe the modeling of curves and surfaces or rigid solid modeling. The reports of the panel sessions are interesting, but they are more likely to frustrate the reader than to be truly helpful. The major benefit of panel sessions is their interactive question-and-answer nature, which cannot be successfully captured in the book. Readers do not have the chance to voice opinions or ask questions of the panel. The presentation of the material is generally good, although each author's contribution is reproduced as submitted, and no attempt has been made to standardize the layout of the papers. A significant proportion of the papers are illustrated in color, which provides a lead for other conference proceedings and books on graphics to follow. The quality of the color reproduction is exemplary. Unfortunately, the cover is flimsy, which has two effects. First, my review copy arrived in a somewhat damaged state. Second, the volume will not stand up by itself on a shelf. I urge the publishers to provide a more substantial cover for future SIGGRAPH proceedings. Anyone who is serious about research in, and the current capabilities of, computer graphics should purchase this volume. It is probably the best single summary of how the state of the art has advanced (at least in the US) during the previous 12 months. Nevertheless, many of the papers seem to be chipping away at the edges of the subject rather than providing substantially new ideas, and this volume probably contains disappointingly few papers that are destined to become classics.   Online Computing Reviews Service
